# Communication Frameworks

Communication is **the transfer of meaning, not words** — and meaning is reconstructed in the
listener's head, never transmitted intact. Most conflict and inefficiency comes from assuming the
message received equals the message sent. This file covers the models and concrete frameworks
(active listening, NVC, feedback, difficult conversations) that close the gap between intent and
impact.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **The transmission model and its failure:** sender → encode → channel (with **noise**) → decode
  → receiver, with **feedback** closing the loop. Noise is physical, semantic (different word
  meanings), and psychological (bias, emotion). The receiver decodes through *their* frame, so
  **intent ≠ impact**.
- **Channels & richness:** face-to-face > video > voice > text in *richness* (nonverbal cues,
  immediacy of feedback). High-stakes/emotional messages need rich channels; text strips tone and
  invites misread (a major source of modern conflict).
- **Verbal vs. nonverbal:** tone, facial expression, body language, and timing carry large
  emotional weight; when verbal and nonverbal conflict, people believe the nonverbal. (The precise
  "93% nonverbal" stat is a misquote of Mehrabian — it applies only to emotional/attitudinal
  messages, not all communication — but the directional point stands.)
- **Listening is active, not passive:** comprehension + signaling understanding + withholding
  premature judgment. Most "listening" is waiting to talk.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Active listening:** attend fully → **paraphrase/reflect** ("so what I'm hearing is…") → ask
  clarifying questions → **label feelings** → withhold solutions until understanding is confirmed.
  Reflecting proves you heard and corrects decoding errors in real time.
- **Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg) — OFNR:** **Observation** (facts, not evaluation) →
  **Feeling** → **Need** (the underlying universal need) → **Request** (specific, doable). Separates
  observation from judgment and defuses defensiveness. ("When X happened (obs), I felt Y (feeling),
  because I need Z (need); would you be willing to… (request)?")
- **Feedback — SBI:** **Situation → Behavior → Impact** keeps feedback specific, behavioral, and
  non-character-attacking ("In yesterday's meeting (S), when you interrupted (B), I felt unheard
  and lost my point (I)"). Pair with curiosity, not verdict.
- **Difficult conversations (Stone/Heen):** every hard talk has three layers — the **"what
  happened"** story (move from blame to *contribution*), the **feelings** conversation
  (acknowledge them), and the **identity** conversation (what it means about each person). Address
  all three.
- **Crucial conversations (Patterson):** make it **safe** first (mutual purpose + mutual respect);
  when safety drops, people go to silence or violence. Watch for the safety cues; restore safety
  before content.
- **Audience adaptation:** tailor message, vocabulary, and channel to the receiver's frame,
  knowledge, and emotional state.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Assuming intent = impact (ignoring the receiver's decoding frame). 2. Using lean channels
(text/email) for rich/emotional messages. 3. "Listening" to reply, not to understand. 4.
Evaluations disguised as observations ("you're lazy" vs. "the report was two days late").
5. Character attacks instead of behavior+impact (SBI fixes this). 6. Not establishing safety
before hard content → defensiveness/shutdown. 7. Solving before understanding (premature advice).
8. Ignoring nonverbal mismatch. 9. Mind-reading / assuming the worst intent. 10. No feedback loop
to confirm the message landed.

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Rosenberg, *Nonviolent Communication*; Stone, Patton & Heen, *Difficult Conversations*;
  Patterson et al., *Crucial Conversations*; Shannon & Weaver (transmission model); Grice's
  maxims (cooperative principle); Mehrabian, *Silent Messages* (with the caveat above).
