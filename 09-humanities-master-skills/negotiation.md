# Negotiation

Negotiation is **structured joint decision-making under conflicting and shared interests**. The
amateur sees it as a battle over a fixed pie (distributive); the master first looks to *grow* the
pie (integrative), then claims a fair share. The discipline blends game theory, psychology, and
communication into a repeatable process anchored by one concept: your **BATNA**.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement):** your walk-away option. *Your power in
  any negotiation equals the strength of your BATNA* — not your need, eloquence, or aggression.
  Improving your BATNA (and understanding theirs) is the highest-leverage preparation.
- **Reservation price & ZOPA:** your reservation point is the worst deal you'd accept (set by your
  BATNA). The **Zone Of Possible Agreement** is the overlap between both parties' reservation
  points. If ZOPA is empty, no deal is rational — walk.
- **Interests vs. positions (Fisher & Ury):** a *position* is what someone says they want; an
  *interest* is *why*. Reconciling interests (often compatible) unlocks creative deals that
  trading positions cannot. ("The classic two-sisters-and-an-orange" — one wants the peel, one the
  fruit.)
- **Distributive vs. integrative:** distributive = claiming value from a fixed pie (zero-sum,
  anchoring matters). Integrative = creating value by trading across differently-valued issues
  (positive-sum). Most real negotiations are mixed.
- **Anchoring:** the first credible number exerts strong gravitational pull (see
  `behavioral-logic.md`). Whoever anchors well (with justification) shapes the range — but a
  reckless anchor outside ZOPA can blow up the deal.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Preparation (where negotiations are won):**
  1. Define your **interests** (and rank them) and theirs (hypothesize).
  2. Know your **BATNA** and improve it; estimate their BATNA.
  3. Set your **reservation price** and an ambitious-but-justifiable **target**.
  4. Identify **multiple issues** to trade across (creates integrative potential).
- **Principled negotiation (Harvard / Fisher-Ury):** (1) separate the *people* from the *problem*;
  (2) focus on *interests*, not positions; (3) invent *options for mutual gain* before deciding;
  (4) insist on *objective criteria* (market rates, precedent, standards) to depersonalize
  disputes.
- **Tactical communication:** **active listening** and **tactical empathy** (Voss) — labeling
  emotions ("it sounds like…"), calibrated open questions ("how am I supposed to do that?"), and
  letting the other side feel heard lowers defensiveness and surfaces information.
- **Value creation then claiming:** expand the pie via trades (I give on what's cheap to me/
  valuable to you, and vice versa), *then* divide using objective criteria.
- **Concessions:** make them slowly, reciprocally, and signal they're costly; never concede
  without getting something (the norm of reciprocity).

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. **No/weak BATNA** (or not improving it) → negotiating from weakness. 2. Arguing *positions*
instead of exploring *interests* → stalemate. 3. Pure zero-sum mindset → leaving joint value on
the table. 4. Anchoring poorly (none, or outside ZOPA). 5. Letting it get personal/emotional
(attacking people, not the problem). 6. Talking more than listening (information is power; you
learn by listening). 7. Conceding unilaterally / too fast. 8. Negotiating without preparation.
9. Win-at-all-costs in a **repeated** relationship (destroys future value — see `game-theory.md`).
10. Not being willing to walk away (the ultimate source of leverage).
