# Behavioral Logic — Cognitive Biases & Decision-Making

Humans are not rational calculators; we are **pattern-matching, heuristic-driven agents** whose
shortcuts usually work but fail predictably. Behavioral logic — the synthesis of cognitive
psychology and behavioral economics — maps these systematic errors so you can *recognize them in
yourself and others* and make better decisions. The goal is not to eliminate intuition but to
know when to override it.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Dual-process theory (Kahneman):** **System 1** (fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional) vs.
  **System 2** (slow, effortful, deliberate, logical). Most errors come from System 1 running
  unchecked where System 2 was needed.
- **Heuristics (Tversky & Kahneman):**
  - **Availability:** judging probability by how easily examples come to mind (overweighting
    vivid/recent events — e.g., fearing plane crashes over car crashes).
  - **Representativeness:** judging by resemblance to a stereotype, ignoring base rates
    (the conjunction fallacy / "Linda problem").
  - **Anchoring:** over-relying on the first number seen; subsequent estimates drift toward it
    (used in pricing/negotiation).
- **Prospect theory:** people evaluate outcomes as gains/losses from a **reference point**, not
  absolute states; **losses loom ~2× larger than equivalent gains** (loss aversion); we're
  risk-averse in gains, risk-seeking in losses. Explains the endowment effect, sunk-cost behavior.
- **Confirmation bias:** seeking/interpreting evidence to confirm existing beliefs; the engine of
  polarization and bad reasoning. **Motivated reasoning** — concluding what we want, then
  rationalizing.
- **Other high-impact biases:** sunk-cost fallacy, hindsight bias, overconfidence/Dunning-Kruger,
  fundamental attribution error (others' behavior = character, ours = circumstance), framing
  effects, status-quo bias, survivorship bias, base-rate neglect.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Debiasing your own decisions:**
  - **Slow down for high-stakes/novel decisions** — deliberately engage System 2.
  - **Consider the opposite / pre-mortem:** "assume this decision failed — why?" counters
    confirmation bias and overconfidence.
  - **Use base rates and outside view** (reference-class forecasting) instead of inside-view
    storytelling.
  - **Checklists and decision journals** make reasoning explicit and enable learning (write the
    prediction + rationale; review outcomes — separates skill from luck).
  - **Reframe in absolute terms** to dodge framing/loss-aversion distortions.
- **Reading others / systems design:** people respond to *defaults*, *framing*, and *social
  proof*. **Choice architecture / nudges** (Thaler & Sunstein) — defaults, simplification, salient
  feedback — shape behavior without removing freedom (ethically applied).
- **Ethical guardrail:** these mechanisms can persuade or manipulate (see
  `influence-and-dark-psychology-defense.md`). Use them to help people decide well and to protect
  yourself — not to exploit.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Believing *you* are immune to bias (bias blind spot). 2. Using System 1 for high-stakes
analytical decisions. 3. Sunk-cost escalation ("we've come too far to quit"). 4. Base-rate
neglect / ignoring prior probabilities. 5. Confirmation bias in research and hiring. 6. Anchoring
to arbitrary first numbers. 7. Overconfidence in forecasts (no error bars). 8. Narrative/
hindsight bias ("it was obvious") preventing learning. 9. Mistaking de-biasing as a one-time fix
rather than ongoing practice.

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*; Tversky & Kahneman (1974, 1979) seminal papers; Thaler &
  Sunstein, *Nudge*; Ariely, *Predictably Irrational*; Tetlock, *Superforecasting* (calibration);
  Stanovich on rationality.
