# Public Speaking

Public speaking is **the engineering of understanding and persuasion in real time**. The fear is
near-universal, but the skill is entirely learnable because it reduces to a few mechanisms:
structure that aids memory, delivery that conveys credibility and emotion, and rehearsal that
builds fluency. The principles trace back to Aristotle and hold today.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Aristotle's rhetoric — the three appeals:** **Ethos** (credibility/character), **Pathos**
  (emotion), **Logos** (logic/evidence). Persuasion needs all three; over-relying on logos alone
  is the most common educated-speaker error — *people decide emotionally and justify rationally*
  (see `behavioral-logic.md`).
- **Structure for memory & impact:** audiences remember structure, not data dumps. The classic:
  **tell them what you'll say → say it → summarize.** Within that, a clear arc (problem → insight →
  resolution) borrowed from narrative (`../02-media-production/storytelling-psychology.md`).
- **The primacy/recency effect:** openings and closings are remembered most (peak–end rule) —
  invest disproportionately in a strong hook and a memorable close.
- **Cognitive load:** working memory is limited (~"magic number" a few chunks). One core message,
  ~3 supporting points, concrete examples > abstraction. Slides support, never duplicate, the
  speaker (avoid "death by PowerPoint").
- **Delivery / paralinguistics:** vocal variety (pitch, pace, volume), **strategic pauses** (the
  most underused tool — pauses create emphasis and let points land), and prosody carry much of the
  meaning. Body language, eye contact, and gestures convey ethos and conviction.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **The preparation pipeline:**
  1. **Audience & objective:** who are they, what's their state, what *one* thing should change?
  2. **Core message:** a single sentence everything serves.
  3. **Structure:** hook → ~3 points (each with a story/example/data) → memorable close + call to
     action.
  4. **Open strong:** a question, story, surprising fact, or vivid image — never "today I'm going
     to talk about…".
  5. **Rehearse out loud**, ideally recorded or to a person — fluency comes from reps, not from
     re-reading slides.
- **Storytelling as the vehicle:** wrap points in narrative — concrete characters and stakes
  drive engagement and retention far better than bullet lists (the simulation effect from
  storytelling psychology).
- **Managing anxiety (it's physiological, see `../08-combat-kinesiology/combat-psychology.md`):**
  reframe arousal as excitement/readiness (challenge appraisal), breathe to regulate, prepare
  thoroughly (preparation is the antidote to fear), and focus outward on serving the audience, not
  inward on self-evaluation.
- **Read and adapt:** watch the room; adjust pace/energy; invite engagement. A speech is a
  relationship, not a recital.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. **Logos-only** (data dump, no emotion/story/credibility). 2. No clear single message — trying
to say everything, landing nothing. 3. Weak open/close (squandering primacy/recency). 4. Reading
slides / slides as teleprompter. 5. Monotone delivery, no pauses, rushing (fear-driven speed).
6. Information overload (exceeding cognitive load). 7. Under-rehearsing (or memorizing word-for-
word, which sounds robotic and shatters if you lose your place). 8. Inward self-focus amplifying
anxiety. 9. Ignoring the audience's state/level. 10. No call to action — leaving the audience
without a next step.
