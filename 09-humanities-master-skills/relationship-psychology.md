# Relationship Psychology

Close relationships are among the strongest predictors of happiness, health, and longevity (the
Harvard Study of Adult Development found relationship quality outpredicts wealth or fame). Far
from mysterious, intimate bonds follow **studied, replicable patterns** — attachment, emotional
attunement, and conflict dynamics — that determine whether relationships thrive or dissolve.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth; Hazan & Shaver for adults):** early bonds shape an
  internal working model of intimacy → adult styles: **secure** (comfortable with closeness +
  autonomy), **anxious** (fear of abandonment, hyperactivating), **avoidant** (discomfort with
  dependence, deactivating), **disorganized** (mixed). Styles are tendencies, *not fixed* —
  "earned secure" attachment is achievable. Anxious-avoidant pairings create a common pursue-
  withdraw trap.
- **Gottman's findings (decades of observational research):** the **Four Horsemen** that predict
  dissolution — **criticism** (attacking character vs. complaint about behavior), **contempt**
  (the single strongest predictor of divorce — disdain, mockery), **defensiveness** (counter-
  attacking/victim stance), and **stonewalling** (shutting down/withdrawing). Antidotes: gentle
  start-up, building a culture of appreciation, taking responsibility, and physiological
  self-soothing.
- **The magic ratio:** stable relationships maintain roughly **5:1 positive-to-negative**
  interactions during conflict. It's not the absence of conflict but the ratio and repair.
- **Repair attempts & bids:** partners constantly make small **bids** for connection; **turning
  toward** them (vs. away/against) builds the "emotional bank account." Successful **repair
  attempts** during conflict separate masters from disasters.
- **Emotional attunement (EFT — Sue Johnson):** beneath most conflict cycles are unmet attachment
  needs and fears; de-escalating the negative cycle and expressing the vulnerable emotion
  underneath restructures the bond.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Build the foundation:** invest in **friendship and fondness** (love maps — knowing each
  other's inner world; expressing appreciation). The positive base is what makes conflict
  survivable.
- **Conflict well:** **soft start-up** (raise issues gently, no contempt/criticism), speak in
  **observation + feeling + need** (the NVC/`communication-frameworks.md` overlap), take breaks to
  self-soothe when flooded (physiological arousal > ~100 bpm impairs problem-solving — cf.
  `../08-combat-kinesiology/combat-psychology.md`), and make/accept **repair attempts**.
- **Attunement practice:** turn toward bids; validate emotion *before* problem-solving; express
  the vulnerable need under the surface anger ("I felt scared/unimportant," not just "you're
  wrong").
- **Self-awareness:** know your attachment tendencies and triggers; "earn" security through
  consistent, responsive relationships and (where needed) therapy.
- **Maintenance:** rituals of connection, ongoing appreciation, shared meaning/goals — relationships
  decay without deliberate investment.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. **Contempt** (the top divorce predictor) — sarcasm, eye-rolling, disdain. 2. Criticism of
character instead of complaint about behavior. 3. Defensiveness instead of taking responsibility.
4. Stonewalling/withdrawal (often flooded — needs a self-soothing break, not abandonment). 5.
Trying to problem-solve before emotional validation. 6. Letting the positive:negative ratio
collapse; neglecting appreciation and bids. 7. Missing/ignoring repair attempts. 8. Acting out an
anxious-avoidant pursue-withdraw cycle without naming it. 9. Assuming attachment style is destiny.
10. Avoiding professional help when stuck.

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Gottman, *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work* / *The Science of Trust*; Sue Johnson,
  *Hold Me Tight* (EFT); Levine & Heller, *Attached* (attachment styles); Waldinger & Schulz,
  *The Good Life* (Harvard Study of Adult Development).
