# GMO Engineering

A genetically modified organism has had its genome **deliberately altered using recombinant DNA
or genome-editing technology**. The science is mature and the scientific consensus on the *safety
of approved GMOs* is strong; the controversy is largely socioeconomic, ecological, and regulatory
rather than about food toxicity per se.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Transgenic (classic GMO):** insert a gene from another organism. Tools: *Agrobacterium
  tumefaciens* (natural plant gene-transfer vector), the **gene gun** (biolistics), and protoplast
  methods. A construct includes the **gene of interest + promoter + selectable marker +
  terminator**.
- **Cisgenic/intragenic:** genes from the same/sexually-compatible species.
- **Genome-edited (new breeding techniques):** CRISPR/base/prime editing
  (`crispr-applications.md`) — can produce trait changes with *no foreign DNA remaining*, which
  many jurisdictions regulate more like conventional breeding.
- **Selection & verification:** markers, PCR, sequencing confirm stable integration and
  expression across generations.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Established crop traits:** **Bt crops** (insect resistance via *Bacillus thuringiensis*
  protein — reduces insecticide use), **herbicide tolerance** (e.g., glyphosate-tolerant —
  simplifies weed control but drives resistance management), **virus resistance** (rainbow papaya
  saved Hawaii's industry), **quality traits** (non-browning, longer shelf life).
- **Biofortification:** **Golden Rice** (β-carotene/vitamin A to combat deficiency-related
  blindness/death) — a humanitarian case study tangled in regulatory delay.
- **Pharma & industry:** recombinant insulin, vaccines, enzymes produced in engineered
  microbes/cells — among the least controversial, most impactful GMOs.
- **Livestock:** faster-growing salmon (AquAdvantage), disease-resistance research.

## 3. Safety, Ecology & Pitfalls
1. **Food safety:** major scientific bodies (WHO, FDA, EFSA, National Academies) find approved
GMOs as safe to eat as conventional counterparts — but each product is assessed case-by-case.
2. **Resistance evolution:** overuse of Bt/herbicide-tolerance selects for resistant pests/weeds →
requires refuge strategies and rotation. 3. **Gene flow** to wild relatives/non-GMO crops
(coexistence, contamination concerns). 4. **Biodiversity & monoculture** socioeconomic effects.
5. **Seed IP / corporate concentration** — a core social objection. 6. **Public trust vs.
evidence gap** — communication failures, not toxicity, drive much opposition.
