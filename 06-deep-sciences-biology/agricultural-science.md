# Agricultural Science & Advanced Farming

Agriculture is **applied biology, ecology, and engineering at planetary scale** — feeding ~8
billion people while contending with finite land/water, climate change, and soil degradation.
Modern agricultural science spans soil biochemistry, plant physiology, genetics, and increasingly
data/robotics.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Soil science:** soil is a living system — texture, structure, organic matter, **cation
  exchange capacity**, pH, and the **soil microbiome** (mycorrhizae, N-fixing bacteria) govern
  fertility. Degradation (erosion, salinization, compaction, organic-matter loss) is a top
  long-term threat.
- **Plant nutrition:** macronutrients **N-P-K** + secondary (Ca, Mg, S) + micronutrients. The
  **Haber-Bosch process** (synthetic nitrogen fixation) underpins modern yields — and ~half the
  world's food — at large energy/emissions cost. **Liebig's law of the minimum:** yield is limited
  by the scarcest resource.
- **Photosynthesis & yield physiology:** C3 vs. C4 vs. CAM pathways; water-use efficiency;
  source-sink dynamics. The **Green Revolution** (Borlaug) bred semi-dwarf, fertilizer-responsive,
  disease-resistant varieties → massive yield gains.
- **Water:** irrigation efficiency (flood → drip → precision); the energy-water-food nexus;
  aquifer depletion.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Precision agriculture:** GPS-guided machinery, variable-rate fertilizer/water, soil and yield
  sensors, drones/satellite remote sensing, and ML models (links to
  `../03-computer-science-architecture/ai-ml-engineering.md`) optimize inputs per square meter —
  raising yield while cutting waste/runoff.
- **Sustainable/regenerative practices:** cover cropping, no-till/reduced-till, crop rotation,
  integrated pest management (IPM), agroforestry — rebuild soil organic carbon and break pest
  cycles. Balance productivity with ecosystem health.
- **Controlled-environment agriculture:** greenhouses, **vertical farming**, hydroponics/
  aeroponics — high yield/area and water efficiency for some crops, but energy-intensive
  (lighting); best for leafy greens, not staples.
- **Genetics & breeding:** marker-assisted selection, GMO/edited traits (`gmo-engineering.md`)
  for drought/disease/nutrition — climate-resilience breeding is increasingly central.
- **Livestock & systems:** feed efficiency, methane reduction, rotational grazing; aquaculture.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Soil degradation from over-intensive monoculture/tillage. 2. Fertilizer overuse →
eutrophication/runoff, GHG emissions. 3. Water mismanagement / aquifer depletion. 4. Pesticide
resistance & pollinator harm. 5. Monoculture fragility (disease vulnerability, e.g., banana/wheat
rust). 6. Ignoring smallholder/socioeconomic context. 7. Tech-solutionism without agronomic
fundamentals. 8. Post-harvest loss (a huge, under-addressed waste source).

## 4. Advanced Resources
- FAO reports (<https://www.fao.org/>); Borlaug/Green Revolution history; *Nature Food*;
  USDA-ARS; soil-health frameworks (e.g., NRCS); IPCC agriculture/land chapters.
