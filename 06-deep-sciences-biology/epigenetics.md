# Epigenetics

Epigenetics studies **heritable changes in gene expression that don't alter the DNA sequence** —
the regulatory layer that makes a neuron and a liver cell differ despite identical genomes. It is
how environment and development write onto the genome, and (partly) how some marks pass across
cell divisions and even generations.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **DNA methylation:** addition of methyl groups to cytosines (typically CpG islands), generally
  *silencing* genes when in promoters. Catalyzed by DNMTs; reversible. The most-studied mark.
- **Histone modifications:** DNA wraps around histones (nucleosomes); chemical tags on histone
  tails (acetylation → generally activating/open chromatin; methylation → context-dependent)
  regulate accessibility. The "histone code."
- **Chromatin remodeling:** euchromatin (open, active) vs. heterochromatin (condensed, silent);
  remodeling complexes reposition nucleosomes.
- **Non-coding RNAs:** miRNAs, lncRNAs regulate expression post-transcriptionally and guide
  chromatin states (e.g., X-inactivation via Xist).
- **Genomic imprinting:** parent-of-origin-specific expression (e.g., Igf2) — a clear case of
  epigenetic inheritance.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Environmental epigenetics:** diet, stress, toxins, exercise, and early-life environment shift
  methylation/histone marks → links to `../05-human-physiology-optimization/longevity.md`
  (epigenetic drift is an aging hallmark).
- **Epigenetic clocks:** methylation patterns (Horvath clock) estimate biological age — a leading
  aging biomarker.
- **Disease & therapeutics:** aberrant methylation silences tumor suppressors (cancer);
  epigenetic drugs (DNMT/HDAC inhibitors) are approved for some cancers. Reprogramming (Yamanaka
  factors) resets epigenetic state — a frontier in regeneration/longevity.
- **Transgenerational inheritance:** evidence (mostly animal; human data debated) that some marks
  survive reprogramming and affect offspring — biologically important but often overstated in
  popular media.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Overstating transgenerational human inheritance (most marks are erased between generations).
2. "Lamarckism returns" hype. 3. Confusing correlation (a methylation difference) with causation.
4. Ignoring that most epigenetic marks are *responses* to, not independent of, genetics and
environment. 5. Misreading epigenetic-clock outputs as destiny.
