# Martial Arts Physics

Beneath the styles, martial arts are governed by **Newtonian mechanics**: force, momentum,
leverage, torque, and the management of balance. Understanding the physics demystifies why
techniques work — and why a smaller person can control a larger one through leverage and timing
rather than strength.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Force & momentum:** `F = ma`; `p = mv`; **impulse-momentum** (`FΔt = Δp`) — strikes maximize
  force in minimal contact time; throws/takedowns redirect an opponent's momentum. Striking arts
  *deliver* momentum; grappling arts *manage* it.
- **Leverage & torque (`τ = F × r`):** joint locks and throws apply force at a distance from a
  pivot (a joint or the opponent's center of mass), so small force × long moment arm overcomes
  large muscular strength. This is the core of why technique beats size in grappling.
- **Balance & center of gravity (CoG):** stability requires the CoG over the base of support.
  Throws/sweeps work by moving the opponent's CoG *outside* their base (off-balancing —
  judo's **kuzushi**) before applying technique. Lowering your own CoG and widening your base
  increases stability.
- **Rotational dynamics:** spinning techniques and throws use angular momentum (`L = Iω`);
  reducing the moment of inertia (tucking) speeds rotation. Hip rotation drives most striking
  power (shared with `boxing-mechanics.md`).
- **Levers in the body:** most human joints are third-class levers (force between fulcrum and
  load) — favoring speed/ROM over force, which is why *velocity* and *technique* dominate striking
  output.
- **Pressure & surface area:** `Pressure = Force/Area` — strikes concentrate force on small
  surfaces (knuckles, edge of hand, shin) to maximize pressure on impact.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Striking arts (karate, muay thai, TKD):** maximize end-effector velocity via proximal-to-
  distal sequencing + hip rotation + effective mass; concentrate force (small contact area);
  retract fast (impulse). Conditioning of striking surfaces and alignment to avoid self-injury.
- **Grappling arts (judo, BJJ, wrestling):** win the leverage/balance game — off-balance first
  (kuzushi), then apply technique with mechanical advantage; control the opponent's CoG and use
  your frame/levers, not a strength contest. Joint locks exploit the joint's anatomical limits +
  torque.
- **Distance & timing:** range management (the "pocket"), and timing (intercepting/redirecting
  momentum) convert physics into applied skill. Energy efficiency — economy of motion — wins long
  exchanges.
- **The unifying principle:** *generate, transfer, or redirect momentum efficiently while
  protecting your own balance.* Every style is a different solution to that one mechanical
  problem.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Relying on strength over leverage/technique (loses to skilled opponents and gasses you out).
2. Striking without hip rotation / proximal-to-distal sequence. 3. Poor balance — committing CoG
outside the base (off-balanced and thrown). 4. Telegraphing and poor timing. 5. Neglecting
distance management. 6. Improper striking-surface alignment → self-injury. 7. Wasted motion /
poor economy → fatigue. 8. Treating techniques as magic rather than mechanics (which blocks real
understanding and adaptation).

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Kano, *Kodokan Judo* (kuzushi/off-balancing); biomechanics-of-martial-arts research; Walker,
  *Physics of Karate* (classic studies of strike dynamics); general biomechanics texts (Enoka,
  Hall, *Basic Biomechanics*).
