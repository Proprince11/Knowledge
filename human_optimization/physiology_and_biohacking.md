## 1. Biomechanics & Hypertrophy

### 1.1 Neuromuscular Efficiency

Skeletal muscle force is governed jointly by the nervous system and the contractile
machinery. Two largely independent levers determine output:

1. **Neural drive** — how *many* motor units are recruited and how *fast* they fire.
2. **Cross-sectional area (CSA)** — the physical mass available to contract.

Beginners' early "strength gains" are dominated by neural-drive improvements (Sale,
*Med Sci Sports Exerc*, 1988); cross-sectional hypertrophy lags by ~6–10 weeks.
Trained athletes are nearer the neural ceiling, so further strength comes increasingly
from CSA and tissue stiffness changes.

#### 1.1.1 Henneman's Size Principle and Recruitment

Motor units are recruited in order of size — small (Type I, slow-twitch, low-threshold)
first, then larger (Type IIa, then Type IIx, fast-twitch, high-threshold) as force demand
rises (Henneman, 1957). Implications:

- **High-threshold fibers only fire near maximal effort** *or* when low-load sets are
  taken close to failure (≤ ~3 reps in reserve, RIR; Schoenfeld et al., *J Sports Sci*,
  2017).
- **"Effective reps"** — the final 3–5 reps of a set near failure are where high-threshold
  units do most of the productive work for hypertrophy.
- **Compensatory acceleration** — moving sub-maximal loads with maximal intent on the
  concentric phase recruits high-threshold units earlier (rate-coding mechanism;
  Behm & Sale, *Sports Med*, 1993).

#### 1.1.2 Rate Coding and Force–Frequency

Above ~80–85% of maximum voluntary contraction, additional force comes mostly from
**increased firing frequency** (rate coding) of already-recruited motor units rather than
new recruitment (Duchateau & Enoka, *J Physiol*, 2011). This is why explosive intent
matters even on heavy compound lifts where load is fixed: the *speed of the intent*, not
the bar speed, drives the firing-frequency response.

#### 1.1.3 Practical Tools to Build Neural Drive

| Method | Stimulus | Programming |
|---|---|---|
| Heavy singles/doubles ≥ 90% 1RM | High recruitment + high frequency | 3–5 sets of 1–3 reps, 3–5 min rest |
| Cluster sets | Maintains bar speed across reps | 5×(2.2.2) — three mini-sets of 2 with 15 s intra-rest |
| Compensatory acceleration | Maximal concentric intent at 50–80% 1RM | 5×3 with bar moved as fast as possible |
| Post-activation performance enhancement (PAPE) | Heavy single → near-maximal effort minutes later, capitalizing on raised muscle temperature and phosphorylation of myosin light chains | 1×1 @ 90% → 4 min rest → 3×3 @ 80% |
| Plyometrics + heavy lift contrast | Stretch-shortening cycle priming | 5 box jumps → squats |

References: Tillin & Bishop, *Sports Med*, 2009 (PAPE); Cormie et al., *J Strength Cond
Res*, 2011 (rate of force development).

#### 1.1.4 Length–Tension and Force–Velocity

- **Length–tension relationship**: actin-myosin overlap is maximal near "resting" sarcomere
  length; force drops at extreme shortening or lengthening. Choose exercise *range* so the
  hardest portion of the lift sits near the fiber's strongest length (e.g., the bottom of
  a deep squat for the glutes; the stretched position of a deficit Romanian deadlift for
  the hamstrings).
- **Force–velocity curve** (Hill, 1938): force decreases as concentric velocity rises.
  Strength training (low velocity, high force) and power training (high velocity, moderate
  force) train *different* points on the curve. A complete program touches multiple zones.

#### 1.1.5 Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy

Recent work (Maeo et al., *Eur J Sport Sci*, 2021; Wolf et al., 2023 meta-analysis) shows
that emphasizing the **lengthened position** of an exercise (e.g., deep stretch of pec on
incline DB press, deep ROM RDL for hamstrings) produces **greater hypertrophy at equated
volume** than mid-range or shortened-bias work. The proposed mechanism is mechanotransduction
via titin and the costameric protein complex under high tension at long muscle length.

> Working rule: **the exercise that "hurts" most in the deepest part of the range is
> usually the one that grows the muscle fastest.**

### 1.2 Mind–Muscle Connection (Internal Focus)

EMG studies show that intentionally focusing on a target muscle ("squeeze the chest")
**increases activation of that muscle by 22–60%** at sub-maximal loads (Calatayud et al.,
*Eur J Appl Physiol*, 2016; Schoenfeld et al., *Eur J Sport Sci*, 2018) compared with
external "move the weight" focus. Caveats:

- **Effect collapses above ~80% 1RM** — at heavy loads the nervous system reverts to
  whatever movement strategy is most efficient. Internal focus is a hypertrophy tool, not
  a 1RM tool.
- **External focus produces more force** in athletic and skill-based tasks (Wulf, 2013) —
  for vertical jump, sprint start, or punch, attention should go to the *target* (the bar,
  the line, the bag), not the body.

#### 1.2.1 Building the Connection

1. **Start light.** 30–40% 1RM; the rep can be slow without crushing fatigue.
2. **Tempo discipline.** 3-second eccentric, 1-second pause, 1-second concentric, 1-second
   peak contraction. The pause-and-peak-squeeze is non-negotiable for stubborn muscles
   (lats, rear delts, glutes).
3. **Pre-activation drills.** 1–2 sets of an isolation movement of the target muscle
   (e.g., 15 cable rear-delt flies) before the main compound (e.g., row). This raises
   conscious awareness of the muscle for subsequent work.
4. **Palpation cue.** Have a training partner *touch* the muscle during the set;
   proprioceptive feedback dramatically improves localization.
5. **Visualization.** Mental rehearsal in the warm-up activates the same motor cortex
   regions as physical execution (Ranganathan et al., *Neuropsychologia*, 2004) and
   enhances volitional drive.

#### 1.2.2 Common Failure Patterns to Watch

| Lift | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Bench press | Triceps fail before chest | Wider grip + 5° elbow tuck reduction; pause on chest |
| Lat pulldown | Biceps & forearms cook | Thumbless grip; pull elbow into hip pocket |
| Squat | Quads do nothing, all hips | Heel-elevated front squat; pause at parallel |
| RDL | Lower back limits | Hip-hinge drill with dowel; reduce ROM until hams fire |
| Lateral raise | Traps shrug | Lean torso 10° forward; lead with elbow, palm facing down |

### 1.3 Progressive Overload Engineering

Progressive overload is the *non-negotiable* driver of long-term hypertrophy and strength.
But "do more next time" is too crude — overload has at least **eight distinct levers**:

| Lever | Example |
|---|---|
| 1. Load | +2.5 kg |
| 2. Reps | 8 → 9 at the same load |
| 3. Sets | 3 → 4 |
| 4. Frequency | 1×/week → 2×/week |
| 5. Range of motion | Half squat → full depth |
| 6. Tempo / time under tension | 2-1-1 → 3-2-1 |
| 7. Rest reduction | 3 min → 2 min between sets |
| 8. Exercise difficulty | Push-up → archer push-up → one-arm push-up |

Pull the lever with the highest *signal-to-fatigue ratio* given the trainee's level.
Beginners get away with linear load progression; intermediates need RPE/RIR-driven double
progression; advanced lifters need block periodization.

#### 1.3.1 Volume Landmarks (Israetel et al., RP framework)

For a given muscle group per week, three thresholds:

| Term | Meaning | Typical range |
|---|---|---|
| **MV** Maintenance Volume | Volume to *not lose* | 4–8 hard sets/wk |
| **MEV** Minimum Effective Volume | Smallest dose to gain | 8–12 sets/wk |
| **MAV** Maximum Adaptive Volume | Sweet spot for growth | 12–20 sets/wk |
| **MRV** Maximum Recoverable Volume | Ceiling before regress | 18–25 sets/wk |

Run a mesocycle that ramps from MEV upward week-to-week and **deload** when sleep, joints,
or performance degrade — that's the body signaling MRV is near.

#### 1.3.2 Intensity Prescription (RPE / RIR)

RPE 10 = absolute failure; RPE 9 = 1 RIR; RPE 8 = 2 RIR; RPE 7 = 3 RIR. Anchor most
hypertrophy work at **RPE 7–9**, leaving 1–3 reps in reserve. Failure can be reserved for
the last set of an exercise on isolation movements where injury risk is low.

A simple weekly progression on a hypertrophy block:

```
Week 1   RPE 7         (recovery from deload)
Week 2   RPE 8
Week 3   RPE 9
Week 4   RPE 9.5–10
Week 5   DELOAD: 50% volume, RPE 6
```

#### 1.3.3 Periodization Models

- **Linear** — load up, reps down over a block. Simple; works for beginners and
  short-duration peaking.
- **Undulating (DUP)** — heavy/medium/light intensities cycled within a week. Robust for
  intermediates.
- **Block** — accumulation (volume) → intensification (load) → realization (peaking).
  Standard for advanced strength athletes.
- **Conjugate** — multiple qualities trained in parallel (Westside Barbell). Suited for
  powerlifters with extensive recovery resources.

#### 1.3.4 Recovery Inputs

Hypertrophy is a *recovery-limited* process. Without these, even perfect programming
underdelivers:

- **Protein** ≥ 1.6 g/kg bodyweight/day (Morton et al., *Br J Sports Med*, 2018 meta).
- **Sleep** ≥ 7.5 h with intact deep + REM (see §2.1).
- **Energy availability** ≥ maintenance during hypertrophy phases; deficits ≤ 300 kcal/day
  during cuts to preserve muscle (Helms et al., *J Int Soc Sports Nutr*, 2014).
- **Stress management** — cortisol elevation suppresses mTOR signaling (Schakman et al.,
  *Mol Cell Endocrinol*, 2008). HRV monitoring is a useful proxy.

## 2. Sleep & Nutrition Science

### 2.1 Sleep Architecture

Adult sleep cycles are roughly **90 minutes** long, repeating 4–6 times per night, with
internal composition shifting as the night progresses.

| Stage | % of total sleep | Brain state | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| N1 | 2–5% | Theta + alpha | Drift transition; trivial |
| N2 | 45–55% | Sleep spindles + K-complexes | Memory consolidation, motor learning |
| **N3 (Deep / SWS)** | 13–23% (heavy in first half) | Delta (0.5–4 Hz) | GH release, glymphatic clearance, immune fn |
| **REM** | 20–25% (heavy in second half) | Mixed-frequency, EMG atonia | Emotional + procedural memory, neurogenesis |

References: Walker, *Why We Sleep*, 2017; Carskadon & Dement, *Princ. & Pract. of Sleep
Medicine*, 6th ed.

#### 2.1.1 Why Each Stage Matters

- **Deep / N3**: largest pulse of growth hormone is in the first 90 minutes (Van Cauter et
  al., *JAMA*, 2000). Glymphatic CSF flow clears β-amyloid and metabolic waste (Xie et
  al., *Science*, 2013). Skipping deep sleep degrades recovery from training and immune
  function within days.
- **REM**: cortical reactivation of the hippocampal traces from the day; consolidation of
  procedural skills and emotional regulation. Disrupted REM is associated with elevated
  amygdala reactivity and reduced PFC control the next day (Goldstein-Piekarski et al.,
  *J Neurosci*, 2015).

#### 2.1.2 Architecture Drift Across the Night

Deep sleep loads the **first third**; REM loads the **last third**. This means:

- An evening alcohol binge or late training that disrupts the first cycle disproportionately
  costs deep sleep.
- Sleeping 6 h instead of 8 h crops the **morning REM windows** — disproportionate REM
  loss with even moderate restriction.

#### 2.1.3 Practical Optimization

| Lever | Mechanism | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Morning sunlight** 5–15 min within 30 min of waking | Anchors SCN; sets evening melatonin onset | Direct outdoor exposure (≥10,000 lux) |
| **Consistent wake time** 7 days/week | Stabilizes circadian phase | Same alarm Sat/Sun |
| **Core temperature drop** | Sleep onset requires ~1°C drop | Hot shower 90 min pre-bed; bedroom 17–19°C |
| **Light hygiene** | Blue light suppresses melatonin (Brainard et al., 2001) | Dim < 50 lux for 90 min pre-sleep; warm/red bulbs |
| **Caffeine timing** | t½ ~5 h; binds A₂A receptor blocking adenosine | Last dose ≥ 8–10 h before bed |
| **Alcohol avoidance** | Suppresses REM; rebound fragmentation in second half | None within 3 h of sleep |
| **Late training caution** | Sympathetic overdrive delays sleep onset | Finish high-intensity work ≥ 3 h pre-bed |
| **Bed-only-for-sleep** | Conditioned arousal | Read/work elsewhere |
| **Magnesium glycinate / threonate** | NMDA modulation, GABA support | 200–400 mg, 60 min pre-sleep |
| **Apnea screening** | Untreated OSA destroys deep sleep | Snoring / daytime fatigue → home sleep study |

#### 2.1.4 What the Sleep Data Should Look Like

If you wear a tracker (Oura, Whoop, Apple Watch, Garmin), look for:

- **Sleep onset latency** ≤ 20 min.
- **Sleep efficiency** ≥ 88%.
- **Deep sleep** 60–110 min (varies by age and athletic status).
- **REM sleep** 90–120 min.
- **Awakenings** ≤ 5–6 per night, none > 5 min.
- **HRV** trending stable or up week-over-week; resting HR within ±3 bpm of personal
  baseline.

Trackers underestimate deep sleep absolute values; trust the trend, not the number.

### 2.2 Macronutrient Targeting

#### 2.2.1 Energy Balance First

```
Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) = BMR + TEF + EAT + NEAT
```

- **BMR** Basal metabolic rate (Mifflin-St Jeor):
  Men: `10·kg + 6.25·cm − 5·age + 5`
  Women: `10·kg + 6.25·cm − 5·age − 161`
- **TEF** Thermic effect of food (~10% of intake; protein highest at 20–30%).
- **EAT** Exercise activity thermogenesis.
- **NEAT** Non-exercise activity thermogenesis (often the biggest variable; collapses
  during cuts).

Hypertrophy phases: surplus of **+5 to +15%** of TDEE. Lean cuts: deficit of **−15 to
−25%** of TDEE. Aggressive cuts beyond that pay in muscle and hormonal disruption.

#### 2.2.2 Protein

- **1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight/day** is the established hypertrophy band (Morton et al.,
  meta-analysis, 2018).
- **Cutting** raises protein needs to **2.3–3.1 g/kg fat-free mass** to preserve LBM
  (Helms et al., 2014).
- Distribute across **4–5 meals**, each delivering **0.4–0.55 g/kg** to maximally stimulate
  muscle protein synthesis (MPS) per meal (the "leucine threshold" of ~2.5–3 g leucine).
- Highest-quality sources by leucine density: whey isolate, beef, eggs, dairy, soy
  isolate. Plant-based diets need 25–40% higher total intake to match.
- Pre-sleep **casein 30–40 g** (Res et al., *Med Sci Sports Exerc*, 2012) extends
  overnight MPS.

#### 2.2.3 Carbohydrate

- Hypertrophy: **3–6 g/kg/day** depending on training volume.
- Endurance / heavy combat training: **6–10 g/kg/day**.
- Around-training carbs improve session quality and replenish muscle glycogen
  (Ivy, *J Appl Physiol*, 1988; Areta et al., 2014).
- Glycemic-index strategy: **higher-GI peri-workout** (rice, oats, fruit, dextrose);
  **lower-GI** the rest of the day for stable blood glucose.

#### 2.2.4 Fat

- **0.6–1.0 g/kg/day** minimum. Below this, testosterone and steroid hormones decline
  (Volek et al., *J Appl Physiol*, 1997).
- **Saturated : monounsaturated : polyunsaturated** ratio approximately **1 : 2 : 1**.
- **Omega-3 EPA + DHA** at **2–3 g/day** combined — anti-inflammatory, supports MPS at
  high age (Smith et al., *Clin Sci*, 2011), and improves recovery.
- Minimize industrial trans fats (any source listing partially hydrogenated oils).

#### 2.2.5 Hydration

- **35 mL/kg/day** baseline; add **0.5–1 L per hour** of intense training.
- Sodium **3–6 g/day** for hard trainers (cramp prevention; plasma volume).
- Urine pale yellow to clear post-first-void; not "dilute" all day (over-hydration).

### 2.3 Micronutrient Targeting

The micronutrients most often deficient in athletic populations and most directly tied to
performance:

| Nutrient | Role | RDA | Athletic target | Best food sources |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D₃** | Steroidogenesis, immunity, calcium uptake | 600–800 IU | **2000–5000 IU** (titrate to 40–60 ng/mL serum 25-OH-D) | Sun (15–30 min skin); fatty fish; eggs |
| **Magnesium** | 600+ enzymatic reactions; ATP cofactor; sleep | 320–420 mg | **400–500 mg** (glycinate, malate, threonate) | Pumpkin seeds, almonds, dark chocolate, leafy greens |
| **Zinc** | Testosterone, immune, wound healing | 8–11 mg | 15–25 mg (don't exceed 40 mg long term) | Oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds |
| **Iron** | O₂ transport (Hb, Mb); critical for women athletes | 8 mg M / 18 mg F | Test ferritin annually | Red meat, organ meats, lentils + vit-C |
| **B12** | Methylation, RBC formation, neurological function | 2.4 µg | 5–10 µg (more if vegan, methyl-B12) | Meat, fish, eggs, dairy |
| **Omega-3 EPA/DHA** | Cell-membrane fluidity, anti-inflammatory | — | **2–3 g/day** combined EPA+DHA | Fatty fish; quality fish or algal oil |
| **Creatine monohydrate** | PCr buffer; cognitive performance | — | **3–5 g/day**, no loading needed | Red meat, fish |
| **Vitamin K₂ (MK-7)** | Calcium routing to bone, away from arteries | — | 90–180 µg | Natto, fermented dairy, egg yolks |
| **Choline** | Acetylcholine, methyl donor | 425–550 mg | 600 mg | Egg yolks, beef liver |
| **Iodine** | Thyroid hormone synthesis | 150 µg | 150–300 µg | Iodized salt, kelp, dairy |

Test, don't guess. A basic annual panel: CBC, comprehensive metabolic, lipid, ferritin,
25-OH vitamin D, magnesium RBC, fasting insulin, HbA1c, total + free testosterone, SHBG,
TSH + free T4, hsCRP. Outliers drive decisions; population RDAs do not.

### 2.4 Adaptogenic Pharmacology

**Adaptogens** are plant-derived compounds that purportedly broaden the body's tolerance to
stress without crashing it on either side. Modern pharmacology classifies them by their
primary mechanism:

| Herb | Active compounds | Mechanism (current evidence) | Practical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ashwagandha** *(Withania somnifera)* | Withanolides (esp. Withaferin A, withanolide A) | GABAergic modulation; cortisol suppression in chronically stressed; mild thyroid support; sub-chronic anxiolysis; possible androgen support | 300–600 mg KSM-66 or Sensoril daily, 8–12 wk cycles |
| **Rhodiola rosea** | Rosavins, salidroside | Catecholaminergic / serotonergic modulation; reduced fatigue under stress | 200–400 mg standardized to 3% rosavins, mornings |
| **Panax ginseng** | Ginsenosides (Rb, Rg) | NO synthase + glucocorticoid pathway modulation; energy + cognition | 200–400 mg extract |
| **Eleuthero** *(Siberian ginseng)* | Eleutherosides | Mild HPA-axis modulation, immune support | 300–1200 mg/day |
| **Cordyceps militaris** | Cordycepin, polysaccharides | ATP and VO₂max support in trained subjects (Hirsch et al., 2017) | 1–3 g extract pre-training |
| **Reishi** *(Ganoderma lucidum)* | Triterpenoids, β-glucans | Anxiolytic, immune modulation, sleep latency | 1–3 g, evenings |
| **Lion's Mane** *(Hericium erinaceus)* | Hericenones, erinacines | NGF / BDNF upregulation; cognitive support | 500–1500 mg fruiting body extract |
| **Holy basil (Tulsi)** | Eugenol, ursolic acid | Cortisol reduction, blood sugar modulation | 300–600 mg / 1–2 tsp tea |
| **Schisandra chinensis** | Schisandrins | Liver protection; CNS stimulation | 500–1000 mg |
| **Bacopa monnieri** | Bacosides | Cholinergic; memory + anxiolysis (12-wk minimum) | 300–600 mg standardized to 50% bacosides |

#### 2.4.1 Stacking Caveats

- **Cycle on/off** — most adaptogens are studied for 8–12 wk windows; long-term unbroken
  use lacks safety data. A simple cycle is 8 weeks on / 2 weeks off.
- **Ashwagandha + medication interactions** — sedatives, thyroid hormone, immunosuppressants.
- **Rhodiola + SSRIs** — additive serotonergic load; consult a clinician.
- **Pregnancy / breastfeeding** — most adaptogens are contraindicated.
- **Quality matters** — buy products with USP, NSF, or third-party COA verification.
  Adaptogen supplement quality variance is *enormous*.

#### 2.4.2 Evidence Quality Reality Check

Ashwagandha, Rhodiola, and Bacopa have the strongest randomized-controlled-trial evidence
in human subjects. Most others are supported by smaller trials or in-vitro/animal data.
Treat herbs as **performance multipliers stacked on top** of sleep, training, and
nutrition fundamentals — never as replacements for them.

References: Panossian & Wikman, *Pharmaceuticals*, 2010; Lopresti et al. (Ashwagandha
RCTs, 2019, 2022); Hung et al. (Rhodiola review, 2011); Ng et al. (Lion's Mane RCTs,
2019).

## 3. Combat Physics & Performance

### 3.1 Kinetic Chains in Boxing & Martial Arts

A strike is not a "punch" — it is a **kinetic chain**: a sequence of summed segmental
rotations that funnels momentum from the largest, slowest body parts (legs, hips) into
the smallest, fastest (fist) at the moment of impact.

#### 3.1.1 Sequence (cross / straight punch)

1. **Rear foot drives** into the floor (ground reaction force; Newton's 3rd law).
2. **Hip rotation** initiates around the longitudinal axis. Studies of elite boxers
   show ~30% of cross power originates here (Filimonov et al., 1985).
3. **Trunk rotation** through the obliques transmits hip torque upward.
4. **Shoulder protraction** delivers the arm forward.
5. **Elbow extension** in the final 0.05 s.
6. **Wrist alignment** locks at impact — knuckles, wrist, and forearm in a straight line.

Each segment fires *just before its predecessor finishes*: sequential summation. Kinetic
chain efficiency is measured by the **time to peak velocity** at the fist — elite pros
achieve fist velocities of **8–10 m/s** in ~150–200 ms.

#### 3.1.2 The Underused Drivers

| Component | What it adds |
|---|---|
| **Rear-leg internal rotation + plantarflexion** | Initiates hip turn; without it the punch is "arms only" |
| **Front-leg post-up** | Forms the rotational pivot; collapses if the knee buckles |
| **Lat engagement** | Drives shoulder forward; without lats, scapula floats and impact diffuses |
| **Exhalation timing** | Sharp exhale at impact stiffens the trunk via intra-abdominal pressure |
| **Fist closure timing** | Closing the fist *only* at impact preserves speed; gripping early slows the chain |

#### 3.1.3 Common Energy Leaks

- **Telegraphing** with the shoulder before the hip — reverses sequence; opponent reads
  the punch.
- **Reaching** — extending the elbow first removes the lat/shoulder contribution.
- **Heel-flat rear foot** — kills ground reaction force.
- **Square hips** — no rotational runway; you lose ~40% of the available chain.
- **Loose neck / lifted chin** — destabilizes the head and exposes the jaw.

### 3.2 Generating Maximum Rotational Force

#### 3.2.1 The Physics

Linear momentum:    `p = m · v`
Angular momentum:    `L = I · ω`
Torque:              `τ = I · α  =  r × F`

Kinetic energy of rotation:

```
K_rot = ½ · I · ω²
```

- `I` = moment of inertia (depends on mass distribution about the rotation axis).
- `ω` = angular velocity (rad/s).
- `α` = angular acceleration.
- `r` = lever arm (distance from rotation axis to applied force).
- `F` = applied force.

Two practical implications fall out of these equations:

1. **K_rot scales with the *square* of angular velocity.** Doubling rotation speed
   quadruples energy delivery. Strength training builds force; **power training builds ω**.
2. **A longer lever arm `r` increases torque** for the same applied force. A taller
   fighter naturally has more reach-leverage; a shorter fighter compensates by maximizing
   angular velocity and impact-point alignment.

#### 3.2.2 Conservation of Angular Momentum

When a fighter draws limbs *closer* to the rotation axis, `I` decreases and `ω` increases
(figure-skater spin effect). For striking this manifests as:

- Pulling the **non-striking arm tightly to the chest** during the cross — accelerates
  hip/torso rotation toward the target.
- Spinning techniques (spinning back fist, spinning kicks): tucking the elbow / chambering
  the leg before extension.

#### 3.2.3 Ground Reaction Force (GRF) Math

Vertical jump and rear-leg drive obey:

```
F_vertical = m · (g + a)
```

A 75 kg fighter accelerating mass upward at 1.5 g exerts **75 × 14.7 ≈ 1100 N** vertical
GRF. Lateral and rotational components stack on top via friction. Higher GRF capacity →
faster hip rotation → faster fist.

#### 3.2.4 Strength & Power Training for Rotational Output

| Exercise | Quality trained |
|---|---|
| Trap-bar deadlift, back squat | Posterior-chain force, GRF capacity |
| Bulgarian split squat | Rear-leg drive; unilateral capacity |
| Med-ball rotational throw (lateral, scoop, standing) | Rate of rotational force development |
| Cable wood-chop / Pallof press | Anti-rotation core (transmission) |
| Power clean / hang clean | Triple extension + tempo |
| Plyometric rotational hops | Stretch-shortening cycle in the transverse plane |
| Speed bag, double-end bag | Hand-eye + chain rhythm under fatigue |

Programming: power & rotational throws *before* heavy strength work, both *before*
metabolic conditioning. **Power qualities die first** under fatigue; train them fresh.

#### 3.2.5 Worked Example — Cross Punch

Consider a 75 kg fighter delivering a cross with a 4 kg arm at 9 m/s. Linear KE delivered
to the target (assuming a rigid wrist and inelastic impact):

```
KE = ½ · m · v² = ½ · 4 · 9² = 162 J
```

If the impact occurs over **5 ms** and the contact area is **3 cm²**, the average force is:

```
F_avg ≈ KE / (contact_distance) ≈ 162 J / 0.05 m = 3240 N
```

That's > 700 lbf delivered to a small area — the reason proper hand-wrapping and gloves
exist. (Note: real strikes also include effective mass from the trunk; elite cross
"effective mass" measured in lab settings is **6–10 kg**, multiplying the energy further.)

### 3.3 Combat Psychology & Reaction Training

#### 3.3.1 The Reaction Hierarchy

Reaction time has three components:

```
Total RT = Sensory delay + Decision time + Motor execution time
```

Typical RTs for trained athletes:

- **Simple RT** (one stimulus → one response): **180–220 ms**.
- **Choice RT** (multiple stimuli → different responses): **300–500 ms** depending on
  Hick's Law: `RT = a + b · log₂(n+1)` for `n` choices.
- **Discrimination RT** (must classify before responding): **350–600 ms**.

Hick's Law (Hick, 1952) tells us that **adding response options grows RT logarithmically**.
The combat implication is profound: drill **fewer high-leverage responses** under pressure
rather than a bloated technique catalogue. Fewer options → faster fighter.

#### 3.3.2 Anticipatory vs. Reactive Defense

- **Reactive** — see punch, slip. RT > flight-time of the punch from ~25 cm out (~80 ms);
  pure reaction is *physically impossible* against a fast, unseen jab. If you only react,
  you lose.
- **Anticipatory** — read the *windup*: shoulder twitch, weight shift, eye dart, foot
  reposition. Anticipation cuts RT to "movement initiation already in progress." Elite
  fighters have moved to evade *before* the strike has visibly extended.

Pattern recognition is built by:

1. **Sparring volume** with varied opponents.
2. **Video study** of the opponent and self — rewatch in slow-motion and note tells.
3. **Feinting drills** — when you understand how feints work, you understand which tells
   are real vs. fake.

#### 3.3.3 Arousal and the Yerkes-Dodson Curve

Performance vs. arousal is an inverted-U (Yerkes & Dodson, 1908):

- Too low: lethargy, slow RT, missed cues.
- **Optimum**: focused, alert, controlled breathing.
- Too high: tunnel vision, motor degradation, frozen decision-making (sympathetic
  overload).

Optimal arousal varies by skill complexity. Combat sports demand **high force + high
discrimination**, which puts the optimum *lower* than pure power events. Hence the
emphasis in elite camps on **breathwork, pre-fight rituals, and visualization** that bring
arousal *down* to the optimum, not up.

#### 3.3.4 Breathing and Autonomic Control

- **Box breathing** 4-4-4-4 in/hold/out/hold seconds: shifts toward parasympathetic; lowers
  HR ~5–15 bpm in 60 s.
- **Physiological sigh** (Huberman, *Cell Reports Medicine*, 2023) — double inhale through
  the nose followed by long exhale through the mouth: rapid CO₂ offload and HR drop.
- **Tactical breathing** between rounds: exhale longer than inhale (e.g., 4-in / 6-out).
  The exhale is when vagal tone rises and HR comes down.

#### 3.3.5 Reaction Training Drills

| Drill | Quality |
|---|---|
| Reaction balls (irregular bounce) | Visual prediction + footwork |
| Tennis-ball drop / catch | Simple RT |
| Color-cue mitt work | Choice RT under load |
| Strobe-glasses sparring | Forced reliance on anticipation |
| Numbered combos + coach signals | Discrimination RT |
| BlazePod / FitLight reactive lights | Calibrated RT testing & training |
| Slow sparring | Sequence pattern recognition without ego load |
| Live sparring (controlled intensity) | Final integration |

Periodize reaction work as you would strength: progressive overload (shorter cues, more
options, increased fatigue) over 6–12 week blocks.

#### 3.3.6 The Fighter's Mental Model

A useful operating frame, adapted from Boyd's **OODA loop**:

```
Observe → Orient → Decide → Act → (return)
```

Every fighter is in some stage. The fastest fighter isn't the strongest — it is the one
who **enters the next loop while the opponent is still in their current one**. Feints,
broken rhythm, and angle changes work because they reset the opponent's OODA loop while
yours runs uninterrupted.

References: Hick, *Q J Exp Psychol*, 1952; Yerkes & Dodson, *J Comp Neurol Psychol*, 1908;
Filimonov et al., *Boxing: Means of Improving Striking*, 1985; Loturco et al.,
*J Sports Sci*, 2016 (rotational power); Whiting, *J Sports Sci*, 2002 (anticipation).
