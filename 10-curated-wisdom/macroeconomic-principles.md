# Macroeconomic Principles

Macroeconomics studies **the economy as a whole** — growth, inflation, unemployment, and the
policy levers (monetary and fiscal) that influence them. You don't control these forces, but they
shape interest rates, job markets, asset prices, and the cost of living. Understanding the
*mechanisms* (not the punditry) lets you interpret the environment your finances and business
operate in.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **GDP and growth:** Gross Domestic Product = total output, by expenditure
  `GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)` (consumption + investment + government + net exports). Real (inflation-
  adjusted) vs. nominal GDP. Long-run growth comes from **productivity** (output per worker), which
  comes from capital, technology, and human capital.
- **Inflation:** sustained rise in the general price level (measured by CPI/PCE). Causes:
  **demand-pull** (too much money chasing too few goods) and **cost-push** (input shocks).
  Moderate, stable inflation (~2% target) is the goal; **deflation** is feared (debt burdens rise,
  spending freezes). Hyperinflation destroys money's function.
- **Money & central banks:** central banks (e.g., the Fed) target inflation and employment via
  **monetary policy** — primarily the **policy interest rate** and the money supply. *Lower rates*
  → cheaper borrowing → more spending/investment → growth + inflation; *higher rates* → cool an
  overheating/inflating economy. Quantitative easing/tightening expand/shrink the balance sheet.
- **Fiscal policy:** government **spending and taxation**. Expansionary (spend more / tax less) to
  stimulate; contractionary to cool. Deficits add to national debt; debt sustainability depends on
  growth vs. interest rates.
- **The business cycle:** expansion → peak → contraction/recession → trough, repeating.
  **Unemployment** and inflation often trade off short-run (the Phillips curve — unstable
  long-run). Leading/lagging indicators help read the phase.
- **Interest rates as the master price:** rates ripple into mortgages, bonds (price moves inverse
  to yield), equity valuations (discount rates), currencies, and business investment. The
  yield curve (esp. inversions) is a watched recession signal.

## 2. Application Frameworks
- **Reading the environment:** track the policy rate trend, inflation, employment, and growth to
  understand the *regime* (e.g., rising-rate/tightening vs. easing). This contextualizes borrowing
  costs, asset prices, and job-market strength — useful for personal and business timing of debt,
  hiring, and big purchases.
- **Personal implications:** rates affect your mortgage/loans and savings yields; inflation argues
  for investing long-horizon money (`financial-literacy.md`); recessions raise job risk (→ emergency
  fund matters). You can't predict cycles reliably — *prepare* (diversify, keep buffers) rather than
  forecast.
- **Business implications:** demand, financing cost, and currency move with the cycle; counter-
  cyclical strategy (building during downturns when inputs are cheap) is a recurring edge.
- **Globalization & trade:** comparative advantage, exchange rates, and capital flows link
  economies; supply-chain and geopolitical shocks transmit globally (cf.
  `../06-deep-sciences-biology/agricultural-science.md` for food-system links).
- **Schools of thought (know the lenses):** Keynesian (demand management, active policy) vs.
  Monetarist (money supply primacy) vs. Austrian/others — each emphasizes different mechanisms;
  reality draws on several. Treat single-school certainty with skepticism.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Confusing nominal and real (ignoring inflation). 2. Trying to *time* the macro cycle for
investing (notoriously unreliable — see `financial-literacy.md`). 3. Treating economics as
politically simple/settled (it's contested and regime-dependent). 4. Ignoring second-order effects
(a rate cut helps borrowers, hurts savers, moves currencies). 5. Recency bias — assuming the
current regime (e.g., low rates) is permanent. 6. Mistaking correlation for causation in
macro data. 7. Following punditry/forecasts as fact. 8. Forgetting that *you can't control macro*
— focus energy on what you control (savings rate, skills, buffers).

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Mankiw, *Principles of Economics* / *Macroeconomics* (textbook); Dalio, *Principles for Navigating
  Big Debt Crises* and "How the Economic Machine Works" (accessible model); central-bank explainers
  (Fed, ECB); *The Economist* for ongoing context (read critically).
