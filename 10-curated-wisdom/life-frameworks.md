# Actionable Life Frameworks

This is a curated toolkit of **decision and action models** that compound across a lifetime —
mental models for thinking clearly, systems for getting things done, and principles for directing
effort where it matters. They are tools, not dogma: the value is in *applying* them, and in
knowing which to reach for in a given situation.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms (the core models)
- **First-principles thinking:** decompose a problem to fundamental truths and reason up, rather
  than reasoning by analogy ("how it's always done"). Breaks inherited assumptions; the basis of
  genuine innovation.
- **Inversion (Charlie Munger):** instead of "how do I succeed," ask "how do I guarantee failure?"
  then avoid that. Avoiding stupidity is easier and higher-ROI than seeking brilliance. ("All I
  want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there.")
- **Second-order thinking:** ask "and then what?" — account for the consequences *of* the
  consequences. Most bad decisions optimize the first order and ignore the rest.
- **Opportunity cost:** the true cost of anything is what you give up to get it. Every yes is a no
  to everything else — especially with time, which is non-renewable.
- **The map is not the territory:** all models are simplifications; hold them loosely, update on
  evidence. Confusing the model for reality is a core error (cf.
  `../09-humanities-master-skills/behavioral-logic.md`).
- **Circle of competence:** know the boundary of what you actually understand; operate inside it,
  and be honest about the edge.
- **Pareto principle (80/20):** ~80% of results come from ~20% of causes. Find and concentrate on
  the vital few; ruthlessly deprioritize the trivial many.
- **Compounding (beyond money):** skills, relationships, reputation, and knowledge compound like
  interest — small consistent inputs → outsized long-term returns. Consistency beats intensity.

## 2. Application Frameworks (systems for action)
- **Goals & prioritization:**
  - **Eisenhower matrix:** urgent×important — *do* (urgent+important), *schedule* (important, not
    urgent — where the high-value work lives), *delegate* (urgent, not important), *delete* (neither).
  - **OKRs / clear objectives + measurable key results;** the **WOOP** method (Wish, Outcome,
    Obstacle, Plan — pairs optimism with realistic if-then planning).
- **Habits (Clear, *Atomic Habits*):** systems > goals; make good habits **obvious, attractive,
  easy, satisfying** (and bad ones the inverse); the **2-minute rule** to start; identity-based
  habits ("be the kind of person who…"); environment design beats willpower.
- **Deep work (Newport):** protect long, focused blocks for cognitively demanding work; attention
  is the scarce resource of the knowledge economy. Minimize context-switching and shallow work.
- **Decision-making:** distinguish **reversible (two-way door)** decisions — decide fast — from
  **irreversible (one-way door)** decisions — slow down, gather info. Use **decision journals** to
  separate skill from luck (cf. behavioral-logic).
- **Stoic operating principle:** the **dichotomy of control** — spend energy only on what you
  control (your actions, judgments, effort); accept what you don't (outcomes, others, luck). The
  ancient antidote to anxiety and the most practical philosophy for action.
- **Antifragility (Taleb):** build systems that *gain* from disorder — optionality, redundancy,
  small reversible bets, avoiding ruin. Survive first (avoid the absorbing barrier), then optimize.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Collecting frameworks without *applying* them (knowing ≠ doing). 2. Goals without systems/
habits (motivation fades; systems persist). 3. Optimizing first-order, ignoring second-order
effects. 4. Busyness over effectiveness (urgent crowding out important — failing the Eisenhower
discipline). 5. Operating outside your circle of competence with false confidence. 6. Treating
models as truth rather than tools (map≠territory). 7. Chasing intensity over consistency (missing
compounding). 8. Over-analyzing reversible decisions / rushing irreversible ones. 9. Spending
energy on the uncontrollable. 10. Ignoring ruin/tail risk while optimizing averages.

## 4. Advanced Resources
- Munger, *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (mental models, inversion); Clear, *Atomic Habits*; Newport,
  *Deep Work*; Taleb, *Antifragile* / *Fooled by Randomness*; Holiday, *The Daily Stoic* /
  Epictetus, *Enchiridion*; Kahneman (decision-making, cross-ref behavioral-logic);
  Parrish/Farnam Street *The Great Mental Models*.
