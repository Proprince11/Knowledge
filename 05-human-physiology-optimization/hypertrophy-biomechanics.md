# Hypertrophy & Bodybuilding Biomechanics

Muscle growth is a **stimulus-adaptation** process governed by mechanotransduction — not by
"muscle confusion" or chasing soreness. The evidence base (Schoenfeld et al.) converges on a
few high-leverage variables: **mechanical tension, sufficient volume, proximity to failure, and
progressive overload**, applied through sound movement.

## 1. Technical Mechanisms

**1.1 Primary driver — mechanical tension.** High tension through a full ROM is sensed by
mechanoreceptors and transduced into anabolic signaling (mTOR pathway), raising muscle protein
synthesis (MPS). Loading the **lengthened/stretched position** is increasingly supported as
superior ("stretch-mediated hypertrophy"). Metabolic stress (the "pump") and muscle damage are
**secondary**; soreness (DOMS) is a poor proxy for growth.

**1.2 Net protein balance = MPS − MPB.** Training elevates MPS ~24–48h; net growth needs the
stimulus **and** adequate protein/energy. Without substrate, the signal can't be realized.

**1.3 Volume dose-response.** ~**10–20 hard sets per muscle per week** for trained lifters, with
diminishing returns / "junk volume" past your recoverable ceiling. More is not better than what
you can recover from.

**1.4 Proximity to failure.** Growth is driven by the final reps before failure (high-threshold
motor-unit recruitment). Work mostly at ~**0–4 reps in reserve (RIR)**; constant true failure
adds fatigue disproportionate to stimulus.

**1.5 Load / rep-range.** Hypertrophy occurs across **~30–85% 1RM** *if sets are near failure*.
The "8–12 only" rule is convenient, not magic; moderate reps (6–15) are the practical sweet
spot.

**1.6 Biomechanics.** `Torque = Force × moment arm`. The external moment arm peaks at the
"sticking point"; free weights load by gravity (hardest when the limb is horizontal), while
cables/machines/bands can load positions free weights can't (e.g., peak tension in the stretch).
Select exercises that load the target muscle in its lengthened position with a resistance profile
matching its strength curve.

## 2. Application Frameworks

**Hierarchy (spend attention top-down):** 1) calories + ~1.6–2.2 g protein/kg/day →
2) progressive overload → 3) volume (~10–20 sets/muscle/wk) → 4) ~0–4 RIR → 5) exercise
selection (cover regions, load lengthened) → 6) frequency (~2x/muscle/wk) → 7) rest/tempo.

**Progressive overload:** double progression (add reps in a range, then add load), then add sets
over a mesocycle, then improve ROM/control. **Track every session — the logbook is overload made
visible.**

**Structure:** Full-body / Upper-Lower / PPL all work — choose by recovery and schedule.
Periodize over 4–8 weeks, then **deload** (fatigue masks fitness).

**Recovery is part of the stimulus:** sleep (master variable), protein/energy, and volume
management. Chronic under-recovery = accumulated fatigue, not muscle.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Chasing soreness / "muscle confusion." 2. No progressive overload / no logbook. 3. Junk
volume past recovery. 4. Half reps / ego loading (losing the lengthened position + injury risk).
5. Training too far from — or always to — failure. 6. Ignoring nutrition/sleep. 7. Program
hopping. 8. Neglecting weak regions. 9. Equating the pump with growth. 10. No deloads.
