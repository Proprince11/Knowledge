# Pharmacology of Herbs & Botanical Compounds

Botanicals are **drug mixtures**: a single herb contains dozens of bioactive constituents acting
on real receptors, enzymes, and pathways. Studying them pharmacologically (pharmacokinetics,
pharmacodynamics, interactions) — rather than as folklore — is what separates safe,
evidence-aware use from harm. The single most important fact: *active = potentially toxic =
potentially interacting.*

## 1. Technical Mechanisms
- **Pharmacokinetics (ADME):** Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion determine
  exposure. Many botanicals have **poor oral bioavailability** (e.g., curcumin) and variable
  content (plant chemotype, growing conditions, extraction, adulteration) — so "a herb" is not a
  standardized dose.
- **Pharmacodynamics:** constituents bind receptors/enzymes like drugs — e.g., caffeine
  (adenosine antagonist), nicotine (nAChR agonist), morphine-class alkaloids (opioid receptors).
  Effects are dose-dependent with real toxic thresholds.
- **The CYP450 / interaction problem (most clinically important):** herbs modulate
  drug-metabolizing enzymes and transporters. **St. John's Wort** strongly *induces* CYP3A4 and
  P-glycoprotein → can render oral contraceptives, immunosuppressants, anticoagulants, antivirals,
  and many drugs sub-therapeutic. **Grapefruit (furanocoumarins)** *inhibits* CYP3A4 → dangerous
  drug accumulation. Others affect platelet function (ginkgo, garlic, high-dose fish oil) →
  bleeding risk with anticoagulants.
- **Hepatotoxicity & contamination:** some botanicals are directly hepatotoxic (e.g.,
  comfrey/pyrrolizidine alkaloids, kava concerns); supplements are loosely regulated and may be
  adulterated or mislabeled.

## 2. Application Frameworks (evidence-aware, safety-first)
- **Categories with better evidence (general, educational):** *adaptogens* (ashwagandha —
  stress/sleep signals; caution in thyroid/autoimmune), *anti-inflammatory* (curcumin —
  bioavailability-limited), *nootropic/stimulant* (caffeine — best-studied ergogenic), *sleep*
  (valerian, chamomile — modest). Evidence quality varies widely; effect sizes are often small.
- **Risk-reduction principles:** (1) Talk to a pharmacist/physician about **interactions** before
  combining with any medication. (2) Prefer **standardized extracts** from third-party-tested
  brands (USP/NSF/ConsumerLab) to manage dose/contamination. (3) **One variable at a time**; start
  low; monitor. (4) Respect **contraindications** (pregnancy, surgery — stop blood-thinning herbs
  pre-op, liver/kidney disease). (5) "Traditional use" ≠ proven efficacy or safety.
- **Regulatory reality:** in many regions supplements aren't pre-market-approved for
  safety/efficacy; quality is the buyer's burden — hence third-party testing matters.

## 3. Common Pitfalls
1. Assuming "natural = safe." 2. Ignoring **herb–drug interactions** (the top real-world danger).
3. Unstandardized/contaminated products. 4. Megadosing. 5. Stacking multiple actives blindly.
6. Using herbs to self-treat serious conditions instead of seeking care. 7. Trusting
marketing/anecdote over evidence. 8. Not disclosing supplement use to your doctor. 9. Continuing
blood-thinning herbs before surgery.
